              Janaki Ammal was a renowned Indian botanist born on November 4, 1897, in Thalassery, Kerala.
              Her full name was Edavaleth Kakkat Janaki Ammal. Here are some key details about her life and
              achievements:

              Family Background: Janaki Ammal was born into a cultured middle-class family. Her father,
              Edavalath Kakkat Krishnan, was a Diwan Bahadur and Deputy Collector in the Malabar district
              of the Madras Presidency. Her mother, Devi Kuruvayi, was the daughter of John Child 
              Hannyngton, a colonial administrator and Resident at Travancore.
              
              Education: Janaki Ammal completed her schooling in Thalassery and later pursued higher education
              in Madras. She earned her Bachelor's degree from Queen Mary's College and an honors degree in botany
              from Presidency College. She then moved to the University of Michigan, where she earned a Master's 
              degree in botany in 1926 and a Ph.D. in 1931.
              
              Career: Janaki Ammal made significant contributions to the fields of genetics, evolution, phytogeography,
              and ethnobotany. Her research focused on plant cytogenetics, particularly in sugarcane and eggplant. 
              She worked at the Royal Horticultural Society's Garden in Wisley and later returned to India to reorganize
              the Botanical Survey of India.
              
              Awards and Recognition: Janaki Ammal was awarded the Padma Shri in 1977 and the Padma Bhushan in 1984 for 
              her contributions to botanical science. She was also elected as a Fellow of several prestigious scientific
              societies, including the Linnean Society of London and the Royal Geographical Society.
              
              
              Legacy: Janaki Ammal's work had a lasting impact on the field of botany, particularly in India.
              She played a key role in improving sugarcane yields and was a pioneer in the field of plant breeding
              and genetics. A species of Magnolia, "Magnolia Kobus Janaki Ammal," was named after her in recognition 
              of her contributions.
